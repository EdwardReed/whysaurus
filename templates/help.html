What is Whysaurus?

It's made of points

Whysaurus helps you explain ideas by dividing them into bite-size points. Each point can be supported or countered by other points, as well as rated and commented on individually.

Points are reusable

When you create a good explanation, others can use it to support or counter another Whysaurus point. When adding a supporting or counter point, you can search the system to see what's already out there.

Creation is collaborative

Point creation is collaborative. Users create and refine points together, to arrive at the best explanation over time. Any user can edit any point, and all work is saved in the history, so that it is never lost and can be recovered.

A repository of explanations

Over time, we hope that Whysaurus will become a reference repository of explanations and convincing arguments for positions and opinions. Together, we can create the go-to place for people who are looking for the best explanation of an idea.

Mission

Whysaurus seeks to connect people who are passionate about reason, to create tools for illustrating the relationships between ideas, to share the world's best explanations and to help us all to organize our thoughts.

What can I use Whysaurus for?

Collaborate

When working with a team, the Whysaurus format allows for collaboration: edit as a team, vote on what works and what doesn't, and reuse already completed work by linking it into your tree.

Make Decisions

Whysaurus can be used to decide a difficult question by building one explanation for the pros and another for the cons. When you present your recommendations to a decision-maker, they can navigate the tree and see the reasoning behind your explanation.

Explain complex concepts

Whether you are looking to consider great philosophical questions or the latest political conundrum, a Whysaurus point can help simplify, clarify and get to the heart of the matter.

Publish your opinion

The internet is moving away from the essay or article format. Why not illustrate your thesis as a tree of Whysaurus points? There is no tl;dr, your readers can focus on the point in your argument that is most of interest, and can help improve it.

Make a convincing argument

Sometimes you just need to argue on the internet. You may want to convince your friends or a recalcitrant family member of an idea you believe in. With Whysaurus, your explanation remains online to be reused, and can be refined and added to by others.

How can I make Whysaurus better?

  • Voice your opinion on points by clicking agree or disagree
  • Make your own points
  • Edit points made by others if you see ways to make them better
  • Recommend Whysaurus to your friends
  • Send us a suggestion for improving the site

What makes a point strong?

  • A strong point is specific, concise and connected to a set of strong supporting points, thus making it convincing.
  • A strong point is supported by linked sources.
  • A strong point is a standalone statement, phrased to be understood in any context

What makes a point weak?

A point can be weak for a variety of reasons. It might be:
  • Vague or unclear without further explanation
  • Based on insufficient or irrelevant supporting points
  • Not supported by sources

Opinions and Subjective Points

Whysaurus can be used to illustrate opinions and subjective ideas. The key is to make sure that you create a sufficient body of supporting points to prove your idea.

How is Whysaurus different than Wikipedia?

Wikipedia collects facts; Whysaurus illustrates the relationships between ideas. Whysaurus is also particularly useful for examining ideas that are topics of debate - grey areas where it's unclear what is true and what isn't. By breaking complex ideas down into a series of bite-size points, Whysaurus helps us navigate the murky waters of uncertainty.

Nuts and Bolts

How is a point's score calculated?

A point's score equals the number of its agrees minus the number of its disagrees. In the future the scores of supporting points may have an affect on this algorithm as well.

If you make a point, you are automatically considered to have agreed with it.

How is the color of the score determined?

The color is based solely on the value of the score; it allows the value of a group of points to be surmised at a glance. The color system is based on the traffic light: green is strong and red is weak. The current thresholds are:The current thresholds are:

3 and higher = green
-2 to 2 = yellow
-3 and lower = red

Whysaurus is a work-in-progress, so expect these thresholds to change as we improve the site. This means that the score colors of points may change occasionally as we experiment.

Can a point be part of multiple explanations?

Yes! For example, a point about wind might feature in one explanation about kites, one about weather and another about sailboats.

Why are there limits on the length of the titles of points?

Shorter point titles are easier to understand. Also, it's easier to identify strengths (and weaknesses) in an explanation that is divided into many bite-sized points. Short titles can also be shared on twitter without getting truncated. If your title doesn't fit inside the limit, consider dividing the idea into several smaller points, or just put some of the text you had in mind into the body of the point.

Best Practices

Goals for this section

This goal of this section is to collect guidelines for how Whysaurus can be used most effectively. It is a work-in-progress. If you have a suggestion for how to improve this page we'd love to hear it! Leave us a note here.

Try to keep each point as simple as you can

A great way to explain a complex idea is to divide it into a series of easily digestible bits. If your point is complex, see if you can divide it into a group of simpler points supporting each other.

Try to write points so that they can be used in multiple explanations

One of the fun things about Whysaurus is how it allows us to see how some fundamental thoughts are part of many different big ideas. If your point is written so that it can "stand alone" - that is it doesn't require the context of other points in order to be understood - then other users will be more likely to link to it in their own explanations.

Note your sources (and illustrate them!)

Readers are more likely to agree with your point if it has good sources. Sources can be books, papers, studies or other websites.

Commenting: Be Specific

Try to make your comments as specific as possible. Whether you agree or disagree, always tells us WHY.

Commenting: Always comment on content, not people

Critical comments should always critique a Whysaurus point but never a Whysaurus user. Comments that break this rule will be removed.

Tone

Whysaurus is a civil community. Please act respectfully and do your best to make the points you encounter stronger.
